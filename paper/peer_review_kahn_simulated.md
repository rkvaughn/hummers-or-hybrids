## 1. Summary & First Impressions

First, I should say that it is a distinct pleasure — and slightly surreal — to see my 2007 *JEEM* paper replicated and extended by two human researchers and a Large Language Model. It confirms my long-held suspicion that if we wait long enough, technology will lower the marginal cost of empirical economics to near zero.

In 2007, I argued that environmentalism had evolved into a form of product differentiation. People weren't just buying transportation; they were buying a "Green" identity. You have revisited this using California's ZEV data and Yale's excellent climate opinion estimates. Your replication confirms my baseline finding: 18 years later, ideology still matters. "Greens" (or at least those in green zip codes) still prefer transit and EVs, and they still shun gas-guzzlers.

The "hook" here, of course, is Elon Musk. You test the "Elon Effect" — the hypothesis that Musk's rightward political turn would cause liberal Californians to abandon Tesla. You find a null result: the correlation between green ideology and Tesla ownership has not statistically weakened through 2024.

This is a provocative finding. It suggests that for all the noise on Twitter (or X), the *hedonic bundle* Tesla offers — range, Superchargers, and status — still outweighs the "disutility" of the CEO's politics.

However, as an economist who thinks a lot about identification strategies, I have some rigorous questions before I'm ready to sign off on the conclusion that liberals are insensitive to Musk's politics.

## 2. What They Got Right

**The Data Construction:**
Merging the Yale Climate Opinion Maps (YCOM) with voting records and Prop 30 data is a significant improvement over my 2007 reliance on Green Party registration alone. Green Party registration was always a "tail" event; your index captures the median voter's sentiment much better.

**The Baseline Replication:**
Your Tables 1 and 2 are comforting. The coefficients on transit and drive-alone commuting behave exactly as urban economics theory predicts. You correctly control for density and income (the "Champagne Socialist" critique is always the first one I get, and you've handled the basics there).

**The PCA Approach:**

**Honesty About Mechanisms:**

## 3. Major Concerns

### 3.1 Stocks vs. Flows (The Inertia Problem)

My biggest concern regarding your "Elon Effect" test is the dependent variable. Are you measuring the *stock* of Teslas in a tract (total registrations on the road) or the *flow* (new car sales in that year)?

If you are using the *stock* (which `log(Tesla BEV + 1)` implies), your research design is biased against finding an effect. A liberal who bought a Model 3 in 2018 still owns it in 2023. They aren't going to set it on fire because Musk endorsed Trump. The stock variable is sticky. To test the "Elon Effect," you must regress *new* registrations on the interaction of Ideology × Post-2022. If you are already using new sales, this needs to be explicitly labeled. If you are using stock, the null result is mechanical.

### 3.2 The Supply-Side Omission

In urban economics, we worry about unobserved heterogeneity. You interpret the coefficient on Ideology as *demand*. But where are the Superchargers located? Tesla (and third-party networks) placed chargers in wealthy, liberal, dense areas first.

If a tract has high Green ideology, it likely has high charger density. If you don't control for the number of charging stations within a 5-mile radius, your "Ideology" variable is capturing "convenience." The "Elon Effect" might be masked because liberals *have* to stick with Tesla because the non-Tesla charging network (Electrify America, etc.) is still inferior in their specific neighborhoods.

### 3.3 Price Elasticity vs. Ideological Elasticity

During your "Elon Effect" period (2022–2024), Tesla aggressively cut prices. The Model Y became significantly cheaper. Basic microeconomics tells us that as price falls, the marginal buyer changes.

It is possible that the "Green" premium *did* fall (liberals liked the car less), but the price drop compensated for it. You are observing equilibrium quantity, not the demand curve shift. Without controlling for model-level vehicle prices, you cannot disentangle whether liberals stayed loyal or whether they were just bought off by price cuts.

## 4. Minor Issues

- **Housing Type:** You control for population density, but not housing type (Single Family vs. Multi-Family). This is crucial for EVs — you can't charge a Tesla easily in an apartment. Liberal areas (Santa Monica, Berkeley) have unusual density/housing mix profiles. A `% Single Family Home` control is needed.

- **The "Light Truck" Definition:** In 2007, a "light truck" meant a Hummer or Suburban. Today, the RAV4 (a light truck) is the best-selling car in America. The stigma of the "truck" has changed. You might want to restrict this category to "low MPG trucks" to keep the comparison to my 2007 paper valid.

- **Spatial Standard Errors:** You mention Moran's I, which is good. But you should probably cluster at the County or Commuting Zone level rather than the tract. Shocks to EV buying are likely correlated across neighborhoods within a media market.

## 5. On the "Elon Effect"

I find your null result on the Tesla/Ideology interaction fascinating.

If I were blogging this, I would argue that this vindicates the **Beckerian model of discrimination**. If liberals have a "taste for discrimination" against Musk, the market price (or quality-adjusted price) of the car must adjust. Your results imply that the *product quality* of the Tesla (the software, the Supercharger network, the range) provides so much consumer surplus that it overwhelms the political disutility.

Or, it implies that virtue signaling has a hierarchy. Driving a zero-emission vehicle signals "I save the planet." This signal is visible to your neighbors. Disliking the CEO is a private sentiment. In the signaling game (as I discuss in *Going Green*), the visible signal (the car) wins out.

---

## 6. What I Would Have Done Differently

If I were writing this paper today:

1. **High-Frequency Event Study:** Move from annual data to monthly data. Look at the 6-month window around Musk's acquisition of Twitter (Oct 2022). Did the *share* of Teslas among *new* EV buyers in Berkeley drop relative to Newport Beach in that specific window?

2. **The "Rivian" Counterfactual:** Explicitly test the substitution effect. Regress `Rivian Share of New EVs` on `Ideology`. If the "Elon Effect" is real, high-ideology tracts should be disproportionately switching to Rivian, even if they aren't abandoning EVs.

3. **The Used Market:** Look at used Tesla prices. If liberals are dumping Teslas, the price gap between used Teslas in San Francisco vs. Dallas should have widened.

## 7. Overall Verdict

**Verdict: Revise and Resubmit**

This is a strong contribution that updates a classic (if I do say so myself) finding. The use of AI to execute the code is impressive, provided you have double-checked the regressions (LLMs are known to hallucinate interaction terms occasionally).

However, to publish in a journal like *JEEM* or even *Regional Science and Urban Economics*, you must address the **Stock vs. Flow** issue. You cannot claim the "Elon Effect" is null based on the total stock of vehicles. Fix that, control for charging infrastructure, and you have a paper that tells us something profound about the limits of political consumerism.
